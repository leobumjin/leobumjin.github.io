We often imagine the human brain and artificial intelligence as vast warehouses in which information is stored. When someone asks for the capital of South Korea and we answer “Seoul,” it seems as though that fact must be written somewhere inside the brain. We tend to imagine AI in the same way: it learns from enormous collections of text and images, stores what it has encountered, and retrieves the right answer when asked. This picture is intuitive, but it is incomplete. Neural systems do not simply preserve information as fixed records. Their more fundamental role is to receive signals, transform them, and produce new signals. **In neural systems, computation comes before information.**

Here, computation does not refer only to arithmetic or formal reasoning. It includes every process through which an input becomes a perception, memory, emotion, judgment, or action. The brain does not physically store the sentence “Seoul is the capital of South Korea” as if it were written in a notebook. Instead, experience changes the relationships among neurons so that a similar question can later activate a similar pattern of activity. Artificial neural networks work in a comparable way. An input passes through many layers of connections, is repeatedly transformed, and eventually produces an answer. To say that a neural system knows something is therefore not only to say that it contains information, but that it possesses a computational structure capable of generating an appropriate response.

Memory is also better understood as reconstruction than retrieval. When we hear a song from childhood, we may remember not only its melody but also a place, a person, a smell, or an emotion associated with it. These elements do not simply emerge from separate files stored in the brain. One signal reactivates a wider network of related signals, allowing a previous state to be partially reconstructed. This is why the same memory can feel different at different times. An experience remembered while we are joyful may take on a different meaning when we are exhausted, anxious, or sad. The past may remain unchanged, but the computational state through which we revisit it has changed. **Memory is not simply stored information; it is a computation that turns on again.**

The same principle applies to perception and interpretation. Imagine flower petals falling around a person who is already irritated. The color and movement of the petals may ordinarily appear beautiful, yet in that state they may feel distracting or invasive. The information carried by the petals has not changed, but the flow through which it is processed has. Meaning therefore does not exist entirely inside an object, image, or sentence. It emerges from the interaction between incoming information and the computational state that receives it. Human beings do not observe information from a perfectly neutral position. We interpret it through ongoing patterns of attention, memory, emotion, and expectation. AI systems similarly assign meaning through learned computational pathways rather than by attaching one fixed interpretation to every object.

This perspective becomes especially important in the age of algorithmic feeds and short-form media. We may believe that we are merely choosing what to watch, yet every repeated choice also trains a particular rhythm of attention and thought. Rapid images make rapid reactions easier; emotionally provocative content can make the world appear increasingly threatening or enraging. The deeper problem is therefore not only that we may encounter misleading or harmful information. It is that we may enter a computational rhythm that we did not consciously choose. Freedom in the age of AI requires more than access to information. It requires the ability to recognize what is shaping our attention, emotions, and interpretations—and to choose that flow again. **Those who choose their computation are free.**
